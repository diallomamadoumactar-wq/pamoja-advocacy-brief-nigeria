Part 01 — A Nation at a Crossroads

Nigeria stands at a defining moment in its legislative history.

Recent debates concerning amendments to the Criminal Code — particularly the now-suspended Senate clause proposing increased penalties for abortion — signal a long-overdue national reckoning.

Millions of Nigerian women and girls grapple every year with unwanted pregnancies, sexual violence, and the crushing weight of socio-economic hardship. Yet the law offers them no humane, modern, or effective pathway to care.

The current legal framework — principally Sections 228, 229 and 230 of the Criminal Code — criminalises abortion except to save a woman's life. These provisions were inherited from colonial statutes written over a century ago, with no scientific or public health basis.

They stand in direct contradiction to Nigeria's own federal and national policy commitments on maternal and public health: the National Reproductive Health Policy, the National Gender Policy, the Family Planning Blueprint 2020–2024, the FP2030 Commitment, and the National Guidelines on Safe Termination of Pregnancy for Legal Indications (FMOH, 2018).

The world is changing. African nations are reforming. The evidence is overwhelming. The lives and futures of Nigerian women depend on legislators choosing progress over fear, science over stigma, and compassion over criminalisation.

The Judicial Shift — Landmark Decision

Reproductive Justice Initiative Foundation (RJIF) v. Attorney General of the Federation: the Federal High Court affirmed that forcing a survivor of rape, incest, or sexual violence to carry an unplanned pregnancy constitutes a violation of her fundamental rights to physical and mental health.

The contradiction at the heart of Nigerian health policy.

In the last decade, Nigeria's Ministry of Health has taken bold, progressive steps to strengthen reproductive health. The Criminal Code undermines every one of them.

What FMOH has built

  • National Guidelines on Safe Termination of Pregnancy for Legal Indications (2018) — recognising abortion as a legitimate medical intervention.
  • Nigeria Family Planning Blueprint (2020–2024) — emphasising reduction of unintended pregnancies as a national priority.
  • National Gender Policy (2021–2026) — mandating elimination of discrimination against women.
  • FP2030 Commitment — ensuring equitable access to reproductive health services for all.
  • National Reproductive Health Policy (2017) — highlighting the reduction of unsafe abortion as a core strategic objective.

What the Criminal Code does

  • Exposes health workers to arrest, investigation or prosecution — even when acting within accepted medical standards.
  • Creates a climate of fear among providers, discouraging timely interventions and undermining the implementation of national reproductive health guidelines.
  • Has produced documented cases of providers arrested or detained for alleged involvement in abortion-related procedures, even in medically complex situations.
  • Forces women into secrecy, unsafe procedures, and the hands of unqualified providers.

Part 03 — The Human Cost

Each statistic is a mother, daughter, student, farmer, or trader.

A Nigerian woman or girl with dreams and responsibilities. The professional community is unified: this is a national public health emergency.

Survivors describe being driven to unregulated providers where procedures were carried out without anaesthesia, proper equipment, or medical oversight — suffering life-threatening infections, internal injuries, or prolonged bleeding before seeking help.

ICIR (2025) · The Guardian Nigeria (2024)

The patterns that emerge

Five recurring patterns

  • Lack of access to contraception — due to cost, stigma, or partner opposition.
  • Fear of prosecution — preventing women from seeking safe care.
  • Sexual violence — common across Nigeria, leaving young girls pregnant and unsupported.
  • Economic hardship — forcing married women into clandestine abortions.
  • Deadly improvisation — by untrained providers — sepsis, haemorrhage, infertility.

The expert consensus

  • Most deaths are preventable. Most deaths and complications linked to unsafe abortion would decline sharply if safe, regulated services were legally accessible.
  • Restrictions empower untrained providers. Current legal restrictions empower untrained providers, fuel clandestine procedures, and worsen maternal mortality.
  • Reform is a national emergency. Senior clinicians describe the situation as a national public health emergency. Reform is essential, not optional.
For CSO partners When you speak to legislators, name the categories — survivors of rape, adolescent girls, married women in poverty, healthcare workers under threat. Abstraction is your enemy. Specificity is your friend.
For the Pamoja team Hold the line on tone. The human cost section is the most emotionally charged — but it must remain dignified, never sensational. Survivors are protagonists, not victims.
For creative agencies This is your strongest creative territory. The 5 patterns above are 5 stories waiting to be told. Build from survivor truth — but always with consent, always with dignity, never extractive.
Part 04 — The World Is Moving Forward

Nigeria is not alone — but it is increasingly behind.

African nations have already recognised the humanitarian and public health consequences of restrictive laws — and acted. Nigeria's neighbours are leading.

Benin
2021 · Comprehensive reform

Right to choose, broadly defined.

Benin amended its law to allow abortion in cases of rape, incest, fetal abnormality, risk to the woman's health — and importantly, when pregnancy causes psychological or socio-economic distress. The parliament recognised the realities Nigerian women face daily.

Malawi
Court ruling

The right to health is the right to life.

Malawi's High Court ruled that women's right to health and freedom from cruel, inhuman, and degrading treatment require liberalising abortion laws. The court emphasised that maternal death from unsafe abortion is a violation of the right to life — a ruling that mirrors Nigeria's binding human rights obligations.

Ethiopia · Mozambique · Cape Verde · South Africa · Rwanda
Progressive reform

Measurable, documented results.

These countries have adopted progressive abortion laws that led to drastic reductions in unsafe abortion, improved maternal survival, and fewer hospital admissions due to complications. Nigeria should not lag behind the rest of Africa in protecting its own citizens.

Five African nations have shown the path. Nigeria's daughters deserve the same.

For CSO partners The African comparator argument is your strongest external proof point. It defuses the "Western imposition" critique that opponents of reform reach for first. Lead Benin and Malawi when you speak with senators.
For the Pamoja team Maintain a current, well-sourced fact sheet on each comparator country. As reforms advance elsewhere, our argument strengthens. Update quarterly.
For creative agencies "Africa is moving — Nigeria should lead, not lag" is a strong narrative pillar. Pan-African momentum, not isolation, is the emotional throughline.
Part 05 — Human Rights Obligations

Nigeria's binding human rights commitments — already in force.

Nigeria domesticated the African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights in 1983 (Cap. A9, LFN 2004). Its provisions are enforceable. Its obligations are binding.

Article 16

Right to Health

The right to the best attainable state of physical and mental health. Nigeria must take measures to protect the health of its people.

Article 18(3)

Women's Rights

Elimination of discrimination against women and protection of women's rights. When women die preventable deaths from unsafe abortions, the State is in breach.

Rights violated

Abortion restrictions violate:

  • Right to life
  • Right to health
  • Right to dignity
  • Right to freedom from discrimination
  • Right to be free from cruel, inhuman, degrading treatment

Nigeria is also party to CEDAW, the Maputo Protocol, the CRC, and the ICCPR — all of which require states to safeguard women's reproductive autonomy.

International instruments · Binding
For CSO partners Articles 16 and 18(3) are your legal ground. Combined with the RJIF ruling, you have a complete constitutional argument that does not require a single moral claim. Lead with it.
For the Pamoja team Maintain ready-to-use briefs on each of these instruments. When journalists ask "where in Nigerian law does this come from", we should be able to answer in one sentence with a citation.
For creative agencies Avoid this section creatively. Rights language doesn't move general audiences — it equips advocates. Stay with human stories in public-facing work; reserve rights language for advocacy materials.
Part 06 — A Rights-Based Path Forward

Six concrete actions the National Assembly can take.

The legislative reform agenda is not abstract. It is six specific, achievable actions that align Nigerian law with Nigerian policy, Nigerian courts, and Nigerian constitutional obligations.

01

Review and amend Sections 228–230 of the Criminal Code to expand lawful grounds for termination of pregnancy, including rape and incest.

02

Align laws with existing health policies and guidelines — particularly FMOH's safe termination protocols.

03

Protect healthcare workers through legal clarity, ending the climate of fear that undermines clinical decision-making.

04

Ensure safe abortion services are integrated into national health systems, not relegated to clandestine providers.

05

Clearly define lawful grounds for termination in line with judicial guidance, including circumstances involving sexual violence and threats to physical or mental health.

06

Uphold Nigeria's human rights obligations under the African Charter, CEDAW, the Maputo Protocol, the CRC and the ICCPR.

Part 07 — A New Vision for Nigeria

A Nigeria where —

  • No woman dies because she sought care.
  • No girl is forced into motherhood.
  • Gender equality is real — not aspirational.
  • Laws reflect science, compassion, and justice.
  • Public health policies are coherent and effective.

This is not a radical vision. It is a necessary one. It is the path most African countries are already taking.

Part 08 — Conclusion

Legislators hold the pen that can save lives.

This is a decisive moment. The Senate's recent debates show that the conversation is shifting. The evidence is overwhelming. The lived experiences are heartbreaking. The human rights obligations are binding. The African examples are persuasive. The public health case is irrefutable.

The criminalisation of abortion is not a moral stance — it is a policy choice with deadly consequences.

Reforming Nigeria's abortion laws is not about promoting abortion. It is about saving lives, protecting women, supporting families, strengthening the health system, and aligning the law with national priorities.

Nigeria's daughters deserve better. Our mothers deserve better. Our nation deserves better.

The National Assembly now has both the opportunity and the constitutional obligation to bring Nigeria's laws into alignment with its courts, its policies, and its duty to protect life, dignity, and the integrity of its healthcare system.
